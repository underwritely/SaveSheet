How to Make a Budget Spreadsheet You'll Actually Use (2026 Guide)

Updated June 2026 · 6 min read

A budget spreadsheet is the cheapest, most flexible way to take control of your money. No subscription, no bank logins, no app deciding what your categories should be — just a simple sheet that shows where your money goes and what's left. This guide shows you exactly how to build one in Excel or Google Sheets, even if you've never written a formula.

The 5 sections every budget spreadsheet needs

Forget complicated templates with 20 tabs. A budget you'll actually keep using has just five parts:

  1. Income — every source of money coming in this month, after tax.
  2. Fixed expenses — rent, loan payments, insurance, subscriptions. Same every month.
  3. Variable expenses — groceries, fuel, eating out, fun. These move around.
  4. Savings & debt — what you set aside or pay down. Treat this like a bill.
  5. Dashboard — one small summary: income minus everything, and how much is left.

Step by step: build it in 15 minutes

1. List your income

In column A write each income source; in column B the amount. At the bottom, total it with =SUM(B2:B5). This is your starting number — everything else is spending it.

2. List fixed and variable expenses

Make two small tables: one for fixed bills, one for variable spending. Give each a category and a planned amount, and total each with =SUM(). Add an "actual" column next to "planned" so you can compare what you expected to what really happened.

3. Build the dashboard

In a clean area at the top, create three cells:

Total income − Total expenses − Total savings = Money left

If that number is positive, you have a plan. If it's negative, your spreadsheet just saved you from an overdraft — adjust the variable categories until it balances.

4. Add a little automation

Use conditional formatting to turn the "money left" cell red when it drops below zero, and green when you're on track. This one visual cue does more for your habits than any motivational quote.

Use the 50/30/20 rule as a starting point

Not sure how much to put in each bucket? The 50/30/20 rule is a clean default:

BucketShare of take-home payExamples
Needs~50%Rent, food, utilities, minimum debt payments
Wants~30%Dining out, streaming, hobbies, travel
Savings & extra debt~20%Emergency fund, investing, paying off loans faster

It's a guideline, not a law. High rent city? Your needs might be 60%. The point is to give every dollar a job before the month starts.

The manual entry in a spreadsheet is a feature, not a bug: typing each expense is what makes you notice the $60 of forgotten subscriptions.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Too many categories. Ten is plenty. Forty guarantees you'll quit by week two.
  • Forgetting irregular bills. Car registration, gifts, annual renewals — divide them by 12 and budget monthly.
  • Budgeting income you don't have yet. Plan with last month's actual income, not your best month.
  • Never looking at it again. A budget is a weekly 5-minute check-in, not a one-time setup.

Frequently asked questions

Is a spreadsheet better than a budgeting app?

A spreadsheet gives you full control, privacy, and a one-time cost instead of a monthly subscription. Apps automate bank syncing but charge ongoing fees and hold your data.

Should I use Excel or Google Sheets?

Both work. Google Sheets is free and syncs everywhere; Excel is faster offline. A good template is built for both — so you're never locked in.

What is the 50/30/20 rule?

Spend roughly 50% of after-tax income on needs, 30% on wants, and 20% on savings and debt. Adjust the ratios to your real life.
