The 50/30/20 Budget Rule in a Spreadsheet (Setup + Example)

Updated June 2026 · 5 min read

The 50/30/20 rule survives because it answers the hardest budgeting question — "how much should I spend on what?" — with three numbers you can remember in the shower. Put it in a spreadsheet and it stops being a slogan and becomes a weekly scoreboard. Here's the full setup, with a worked example.

The rule in one table

BucketShare of take-home payWhat goes in it
Needs~50%Rent/mortgage, groceries, utilities, transport, insurance, minimum debt payments
Wants~30%Dining out, streaming, hobbies, travel, upgrades you could skip
Savings & extra debt~20%Emergency fund, investing, retirement, payments above the minimum

Two details people get wrong: it's take-home pay (after tax), and minimum debt payments are needs — only the extra you throw at debt counts in the 20%.

Worked example: $4,600 take-home

BucketTargetThat means…
Needs$2,300Rent $1,500 + groceries $400 + utilities $150 + transport $150 + minimums $100
Wants$1,380Eating out, subscriptions, gym, weekend trips
Savings & extra debt$920$500 emergency fund + $420 extra on the credit card

If real-life needs come to $2,600 instead of $2,300, the spreadsheet shows exactly where the overflow is — and which want gives way this month. That visibility is the whole game.

Set it up in a spreadsheet (10 minutes)

1. Targets from one cell

Put take-home pay in B1. Then three target cells: =B1*0.5, =B1*0.3, =B1*0.2. Change your income once; every target updates.

2. Tag each expense with a bucket

In your expense list, add a column: N, W, or S. Total each bucket with =SUMIF(C:C,"N",B:B) — no manual sorting, ever.

3. Show target vs actual

Three rows: bucket target, bucket actual, difference. Add conditional formatting so the difference turns red when a bucket overflows and green when it's under. One glance, every week, done.

When to bend the rule

  • High-rent city: needs at 60% is reality, not failure. Run 60/20/20 and revisit when income grows.
  • Debt emergency: flip wants and savings — 50/20/30 with the 30 attacking the highest-rate balance.
  • Variable income: budget on last month's actual income, and set the ratios against that.
The percentages are training wheels. The habit — every dollar gets a job before the month starts — is the bike.

Frequently asked questions

Gross or take-home pay?

Take-home. Budgeting gross income inflates every bucket and the plan collapses on contact with your bank balance.

Do minimum debt payments count as savings?

No — minimums are needs. Only the extra above the minimum belongs in the 20%.

What if needs exceed 50%?

Shift the ratio (60/20/20 is common in expensive cities). Keep the system; adjust the numbers.
