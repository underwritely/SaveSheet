Debt Snowball vs Avalanche: Which Pays Off Faster?

Updated June 2026 · 5 min read

Both methods work the same engine: pay minimums on everything, then aim every spare dollar at one debt until it's gone, and roll its payment into the next. The only difference is the order — and that one choice changes how much interest you pay and how soon you feel progress. Here's the difference on real numbers, and how to see your numbers both ways.

The two orders, defined

Debt snowballDebt avalanche
Attack orderSmallest balance firstHighest interest rate first
Optimizes forMotivation — quick first winMath — least total interest
First victorySoonest possibleWhenever the priciest debt clears

Example: same 3 debts, two different plans

Say you carry three balances and have extra money each month beyond the minimums:

DebtBalanceAPR
Credit card A$8,00018%
Credit card B$2,00012%
Car loan$12,0006%

Snowball order: card B → card A → car loan. Card B is gone fast — one account closed, one bill off your mind, and its payment now piles onto card A.

Avalanche order: card A → card B → car loan. The 18% fire gets put out first, so every month costs you less interest than the snowball path.

The trade is plain: avalanche always wins on total interest (here, the 18% card stops bleeding sooner); snowball wins on how quickly you feel a result. The bigger the gap between your highest rate and your smallest balance, the more the choice matters.

The mathematically best plan is the avalanche. The best plan for you is the one still running in month eight.

How to track it in a spreadsheet

  1. List every debt — name, balance, APR, minimum payment. Honesty round: include the ones you've been ignoring.
  2. Set one "extra per month" cell. This is the number that does the work; everything recalculates from it.
  3. Rank both ways. Sort by balance for snowball, by APR for avalanche (or use =RANK() so it's automatic).
  4. Watch the rollover. When debt #1 hits zero, its whole payment joins the attack on debt #2 — that acceleration is why both methods beat paying "a bit extra on everything".

Pair it with your monthly budget (the 50/30/20 setup tells you how much "extra per month" you can actually afford) so the payoff plan and the budget agree with each other.

Which should you pick?

  • Pick avalanche if you're numbers-driven and your highest rate is much higher than the rest — like the 18% card above.
  • Pick snowball if you've quit plans before and need wins to stay in the game. The "extra" interest is the price of a plan you'll finish — usually money well spent.
  • Can't decide? Run both orders side by side and look at the debt-free date and total interest each way. Seeing the actual gap on your own numbers usually settles it in a minute.

Frequently asked questions

Which saves more money?

Avalanche — always, by construction. The size of the saving depends on your balances and rates.

Why choose snowball then?

Faster first win. A closed account early keeps many people in the plan to the end — and a finished snowball beats an abandoned avalanche.

Do I still pay minimums on everything?

Yes, with both methods. Minimums on all debts, every extra dollar to the single target debt, then roll the payment forward.
